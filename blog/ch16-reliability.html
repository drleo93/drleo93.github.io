Chapter 16 — Weibull Analysis, Censored Data, Prognostics, and RBDO

TL;DR

  • The Weibull distribution is the universal tool of reliability engineering. The shape parameter $\beta$ encodes the failure mechanism: $\beta < 1$ (infant mortality), $\beta \approx 1$ (random), $\beta > 1$ (wear-out).
  • Censoring is critical: treating right-censored observations as failures underestimates component life by 2–5×. Always use MLE with correct censoring treatment.
  • RUL prediction without a calibrated prediction interval (P10/P90 band) is not engineering output — the interval drives maintenance scheduling, not the point estimate.
  • Use likelihood ratio confidence bounds, not Wald (Fisher matrix) bounds, when $n < 30$.
  • Always report $B_{10}$ life (10% failure probability) alongside the characteristic life $\eta = B_{63.2}$.

1. Reliability Functions

Reliability is the probability a system performs its required function for a specified time under stated conditions:

$$R(t) = P(T > t) = 1 - F(t)$$ $$\text{Hazard rate: } h(t) = \frac{f(t)}{R(t)} = -\frac{d}{dt}\ln R(t) \quad \text{[failures/time]}$$ $$\text{Cumulative hazard: } H(t) = \int_0^t h(\tau)\,d\tau = -\ln R(t) \implies R(t) = \exp(-H(t))$$

2. The Weibull Distribution

$$f(t;\eta,\beta) = \frac{\beta}{\eta}\left(\frac{t}{\eta}\right)^{\beta-1} \exp\!\left[-\left(\frac{t}{\eta}\right)^\beta\right]$$ $$F(t;\eta,\beta) = 1 - \exp\!\left[-\left(\frac{t}{\eta}\right)^\beta\right]$$ $$h(t;\eta,\beta) = \frac{\beta}{\eta}\left(\frac{t}{\eta}\right)^{\beta-1} \quad \text{(power-law hazard)}$$

where $\eta > 0$ is the characteristic life (scale; $B_{63.2} = \eta$) and $\beta > 0$ is the shape parameter (encodes failure mechanism).

Physical Interpretation of $\beta$

$\beta$ RangeFailure ModeHazard TrendExamples
$\beta < 1$Infant mortalityDecreasingElectronic components, weld defects, casting porosity
$\beta = 1$Random failuresConstant ($= 1/\eta$)External shocks, software bugs, particle erosion
$1 < \beta < 4$Early wear-outIncreasingBearing fatigue ($\beta \approx 1.5$–2.5), corrosion fatigue
$\beta \approx 3.5$Normal wear-outSteep increasePlanned maintenance items, tyre wear
$\beta > 4$Rapid wear-outVery steepStress corrosion cracking, hydrogen embrittlement

The classic bathtub curve of product life: decreasing hazard ($\beta < 1$) during burn-in → constant ($\beta \approx 1$) in useful life → increasing ($\beta > 2$) in wear-out.

Key Life Metrics

$$B_{10} \text{ life (10\% failure probability)}: \quad t_{B10} = \eta\bigl(-\ln 0.9\bigr)^{1/\beta} = \eta \cdot (0.1054)^{1/\beta}$$ $$\text{Mean time to failure (MTTF)}: \quad \mathbb{E}[T] = \eta\,\Gamma\!\left(1 + \frac{1}{\beta}\right)$$

3. Parameter Estimation

Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE)

$$\ell(\eta,\beta) = n\ln\beta - n\beta\ln\eta + (\beta-1)\sum_{i=1}^n \ln t_i - \sum_{i=1}^n \left(\frac{t_i}{\eta}\right)^\beta$$

For complete (uncensored) data, solve the MLE score equations via Newton-Raphson or trust-region methods. For censored data, add censored contributions to the log-likelihood (see below).

Weibull Probability Plot (WPP)

Linearise the CDF: $\ln[-\ln(1-F(t))] = \beta\ln t - \beta\ln\eta$. Plot $y = \ln[-\ln(1-\hat{F}_i)]$ vs. $x = \ln t_i$ where the median rank estimator is $\hat{F}_i = (i - 0.3)/(n + 0.4)$. A straight line confirms the Weibull hypothesis. Slope = $\hat{\beta}$; intercept gives $\hat{\eta}$.

Parameter Estimation Best Practices
  1. Use MLE, not least squares: MLE is asymptotically efficient and handles censoring correctly. LS on the linearised WPP underestimates $\beta$ with censored data.
  2. Verify the Weibull assumption with the WPP before fitting. A concave WPP indicates mixed failure modes or a 3-parameter Weibull.
  3. Report confidence bounds (90% or 95%) on $\eta$ and $\beta$. Point estimates alone are insufficient for design decisions.
  4. Use likelihood ratio bounds over Fisher (Wald) bounds when $n < 30$ — Wald bounds are unreliable for small samples.
  5. Always report both $B_{10}$ life and characteristic life $\eta = B_{63.2}$.

4. Censored Data: The Critical Statistical Concept

Pitfall — Treating Censored Observations as Failures
Right-censored units (not yet failed at end of study) contribute information that $T_i > c_i$ — they should NOT be treated as failures at time $c_i$. Doing so introduces catastrophic negative bias: component life is underestimated by factors of 2–5×. This is the single most common error in reliability data analysis.

Three types of censoring in engineering:

  • Right censoring: unit removed before failure; exact failure time unknown but $T_i > c_i$. Most common in field data.
  • Left censoring: unit failed before first inspection; $T_i < c_i$.
  • Interval censoring: failure known to occur in $(L_i, R_i]$. Common in periodic borescope inspections and NDT campaigns.

The censored MLE log-likelihood:

$$\ell(\eta,\beta) = \sum_{i \in \mathcal{F}} \ln f(t_i;\eta,\beta) + \sum_{i \in \mathcal{C}} \ln R(c_i;\eta,\beta)$$

where $\mathcal{F}$ is the set of failures and $\mathcal{C}$ the set of censored observations.

from reliability.Fitters import Fit_Weibull_2P
import numpy as np

# Failure times and censored times
t_fail   = [850, 1200, 1340, 1650, 1980]  # observed failures
t_censor = [2000, 2000, 2000, 2000, 2000]  # still running at 2000 hrs

# Combine with censoring indicator (0=failure, 1=right-censored)
times  = np.array(t_fail + t_censor)
censor = np.array([0]*len(t_fail) + [1]*len(t_censor))

# MLE fit with censoring
fit = Fit_Weibull_2P(failures=t_fail, right_censored=t_censor,
                     CI=0.9, show_probability_plot=False)
print(f"beta = {fit.beta:.3f}, eta = {fit.alpha:.1f} hrs")
print(f"B10 life = {fit.quantiles(0.10):.1f} hrs")

5. Remaining Useful Life Prediction

RUL estimation is a regression problem where the target is time-to-failure from the current state. Three key requirements that distinguish it from standard regression:

Pitfall — RUL Without Uncertainty
Reporting "RUL = 47 hours" without a confidence interval is useless for maintenance scheduling. The decision depends on the cost ratio of false alarm (unnecessary replacement) vs. false miss (failure in service). Only a calibrated P10/P90 interval enables the correct cost-optimal maintenance decision.

LSTM-Weibull Hybrid for Prognostics

The recommended approach combines a data-driven health index (LSTM autoencoder bottleneck) with a Weibull degradation model:

  1. Health Index (HI): train LSTM autoencoder on new/healthy operating data. The reconstruction error is the HI. HI = 0 (new), HI increases monotonically toward failure.
  2. Weibull trend fit: fit Weibull CDF to the HI trajectory as a function of operating time. Use monotone regression to enforce HI non-decrease.
  3. RUL estimation: the RUL is the time for HI to reach the failure threshold $h^*$, given the fitted Weibull parameters. Propagate parameter uncertainty to obtain P10/P90 RUL bounds.
  4. Online update: re-fit the Weibull parameters as new HI observations arrive (Bayesian update or sliding window MLE).
Lesson — Monotonic Degradation Constraint
Physically, a degradation Health Index should be non-decreasing. Without enforcement, LSTM autoencoders produce noisy HI trajectories that increase and decrease, making RUL estimation unreliable. Enforce monotonicity either by post-processing (PAVA isotonic regression) or by using monotone constraints in the LSTM output head. A noisy HI that occasionally "recovers" confuses the Weibull fit and produces overoptimistic RUL estimates.

6. Reliability-Based Design Optimisation (RBDO)

RBDO replaces deterministic constraints $g(\mathbf{x}) \leq 0$ with probabilistic constraints:

$$\min_{\mathbf{x}} f(\mathbf{x}) \quad \text{s.t.} \quad P\bigl(g_k(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{Z}) \leq 0\bigr) \geq \Phi(\beta_{target,k})$$

where $\mathbf{Z}$ are uncertain parameters (material properties, loads), $\beta_{target}$ is the target reliability index (e.g., $\beta = 3 \Rightarrow P_f \leq 1.35 \times 10^{-3}$), and $\Phi$ is the normal CDF. The double-loop approach: outer optimisation over $\mathbf{x}$, inner reliability analysis (FORM/SORM or Monte Carlo) per constraint evaluation. For surrogate-assisted RBDO, replace the inner loop with a GP that predicts $P(g_k \leq 0)$ directly (see Chapter 15, constrained BO).

7. Key Takeaways

  • Weibull shape $\beta$ is the first diagnostic: measure $\beta$ from field data to identify the failure mode before selecting a prognostics strategy.
  • Never treat censored observations as failures. Right-censored units contribute $\ln R(c_i)$ to the log-likelihood, not $\ln f(c_i)$.
  • Use MLE for parameter estimation; likelihood ratio bounds for confidence intervals when $n < 30$.
  • $B_{10}$ life ($t_{B10} = \eta \cdot 0.1054^{1/\beta}$) is the design-relevant metric — it defines the 10% failure probability point.
  • RUL prediction requires a calibrated P10/P90 interval. Point estimates alone are operationally useless for maintenance scheduling.
  • LSTM autoencoder health index + Weibull trend + Bayesian update is the recommended prognostics pipeline for industrial rotating machinery.
  • RBDO: replace deterministic constraints with probabilistic feasibility constraints. Use surrogate-assisted reliability analysis (GP PoF) to avoid expensive inner-loop Monte Carlo.
