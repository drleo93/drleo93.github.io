TL;DR

  • Bayesian inference (prior × likelihood ∝ posterior) is the unifying framework behind GP regression, logistic regression, and OLS — they are all special cases.
  • The bias-variance-noise decomposition is the single most useful diagnostic: if train error is low and test error is high → high variance; if both are high → high bias.
  • MLE under Gaussian noise is OLS. MAP with a Gaussian prior is Ridge regression. L1 prior → Lasso.
  • Never compute a matrix inverse directly — always use Cholesky decomposition for PD matrices.
  • GroupKFold, not random KFold, for any engineering dataset with temporal or batch structure. Random KFold on time-series data gives optimistic RMSE by 20–60%.
  • A weak Pearson correlation (\(r=0.09\)) can hide a strong physical signal in the wrong coordinate system. Use physics-motivated features and mutual information for nonlinear screening.

1. Probability Essentials

A probability space \((\Omega, \mathcal{F}, P)\) is the formal substrate. For engineering ML you rarely work at this level, but the Kolmogorov axioms define the rules everything else obeys.

Bayes' Theorem — the foundation

$$P(A \mid B) = \frac{P(B \mid A)\,P(A)}{P(B)}$$

In ML terms: let \(A = \boldsymbol{\theta}\) (parameters) and \(B = \mathcal{D}\) (data):

$$\underbrace{p(\boldsymbol{\theta} \mid \mathcal{D})}_{\text{posterior}} = \frac{\overbrace{p(\mathcal{D} \mid \boldsymbol{\theta})}^{\text{likelihood}}\;\overbrace{p(\boldsymbol{\theta})}^{\text{prior}}}{\underbrace{p(\mathcal{D})}_{\text{evidence}}}$$

The evidence \(p(\mathcal{D}) = \int p(\mathcal{D} \mid \boldsymbol{\theta})p(\boldsymbol{\theta})\,d\boldsymbol{\theta}\) is the quantity maximised in GP model selection (Chapter 5).

Laws of expectation and variance

Law of total expectation (tower property): \(\mathbb{E}[X] = \mathbb{E}_Z[\mathbb{E}[X \mid Z]]\). This underlies the bias-variance decomposition and GP posterior derivations.

Law of total variance: \(\mathrm{Var}(X) = \mathbb{E}[\mathrm{Var}(X \mid Z)] + \mathrm{Var}(\mathbb{E}[X \mid Z])\). This splits total variability into within-group and between-group components — critical for hierarchical models.

Lesson — conditional independence
In a SCADA system, sensor readings at time \(t\) may be conditionally independent given the plant state, even though they are marginally correlated. Formally: \(X \perp\!\!\!\perp Y \mid Z\) if \(p(x,y \mid z) = p(x \mid z)\,p(y \mid z)\). Exploiting this is the basis of Kalman filters and graphical models.

The Gaussian and why it dominates

Three properties make the Gaussian unavoidable:

  1. Closure under linear transformation: if \(X \sim \mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{\mu}, \boldsymbol{\Sigma})\) and \(\mathbf{A}\) is any matrix, then \(\mathbf{A}X \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{A}\boldsymbol{\mu}, \mathbf{A}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\mathbf{A}^\top)\).
  2. Closure under conditioning: if \((X_1, X_2)\) are jointly Gaussian, then \(X_1 \mid X_2 = x_2\) is Gaussian with closed-form mean and covariance. This is GP prediction.
  3. Maximum entropy among all distributions with given mean and variance.

The GP posterior mean and covariance formulas follow directly from Gaussian conditional rules:

$$\boldsymbol{\mu}_{1|2} = \boldsymbol{\mu}_1 + \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{12}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{22}^{-1}(\mathbf{a} - \boldsymbol{\mu}_2), \qquad \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{1|2} = \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{11} - \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{12}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{22}^{-1}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{21}$$

2. Parameter Estimation: MLE and MAP

Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE)

$$\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}_{MLE} = \arg\max_{\boldsymbol{\theta}} \sum_{i=1}^n \log p(y_i \mid \boldsymbol{\theta})$$

MLE for linear regression with Gaussian noise gives OLS exactly:

$$\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}}_{MLE} = (\mathbf{X}^\top\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{X}^\top\mathbf{y}$$

This is not a coincidence: OLS is MLE under Gaussian noise. Every time you minimise squared error, you are making a Gaussian noise assumption.

Maximum A Posteriori (MAP)

$$\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}_{MAP} = \arg\max_{\boldsymbol{\theta}} \log p(\mathcal{D} \mid \boldsymbol{\theta}) + \log p(\boldsymbol{\theta})$$
Rule — MLE / MAP / Full Bayes
MethodOptimisesOutputUncertainty
MLE\(p(\mathcal{D}\mid\boldsymbol{\theta})\)Point estimateNone
MAP\(p(\mathcal{D}\mid\boldsymbol{\theta})p(\boldsymbol{\theta})\)Point estimateNone
Full Bayes\(p(\boldsymbol{\theta}\mid\mathcal{D})\)Posterior distributionFull posterior UQ
MAP with Gaussian prior \(p(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \lambda^{-1}\mathbf{I})\) is Ridge regression. Laplace prior → Lasso (L1 regularisation).

For censored data (reliability analysis), the MLE likelihood accounts for right-censored observations:

$$\mathcal{L}(\eta,\beta) = \prod_{i:\text{failed}} f(t_i;\eta,\beta) \prod_{j:\text{censored}} [1 - F(t_j;\eta,\beta)]$$

3. Bias-Variance-Noise Decomposition

The single most important theoretical result for applied ML. It tells you why a model fails and what to do about it.

$$\mathbb{E}_{\mathcal{D},\varepsilon}\!\left[(y - \hat{f}(\mathbf{x}))^2\right] = \underbrace{\left(\mathbb{E}_\mathcal{D}[\hat{f}(\mathbf{x})] - f(\mathbf{x})\right)^2}_{\textbf{Bias}^2} + \underbrace{\mathrm{Var}_\mathcal{D}\!\left(\hat{f}(\mathbf{x})\right)}_{\textbf{Variance}} + \underbrace{\sigma_\varepsilon^2}_{\textbf{Noise}}$$

Bias²: systematic error. Model consistently predicts the wrong value regardless of training data. Causes: wrong model family, missing features, over-regularisation. Fix: more expressive model, better features, less regularisation.

Variance: sensitivity to training data. Different training sets give different predictions. Causes: too many parameters, too little data. Fix: more data, more regularisation, ensembles.

Noise \(\sigma_\varepsilon^2\): irreducible. Cannot be reduced by any modelling choice — only by improving instrumentation.

Rule — Diagnosing model failure
SymptomDiagnosisFix
Train error low, test error highHigh varianceMore data, regularise, simpler model
Both train and test error highHigh biasBetter features, more complex model
Test error ≈ train error, both highIrreducible noiseBetter sensors
Test error ≪ train errorData leakageFix train/test split
Pitfall — Optimal complexity is not universal
In engineering: bias dominates when fitting a linear response surface model (RSM) to a highly nonlinear combustion process. Variance dominates when using a deep MLP with 200 training points. The right model for the problem depends on your specific \(n\) and the true function complexity — not on general intuitions about "deep learning is better".

4. Information Theory Essentials

Entropy and KL Divergence

Shannon entropy: \(H(p) = -\mathbb{E}_p[\log p(x)]\). For \(\mathcal{N}(\mu,\sigma^2)\): \(H = \frac{1}{2}\log(2\pi e \sigma^2)\). More variance → more entropy.

KL divergence from \(q\) to \(p\): \(D_{KL}(p \| q) = \int p(x)\log\frac{p(x)}{q(x)}\,dx \geq 0\), with equality iff \(p = q\). Minimised in variational inference when approximating the true posterior \(p\) with simpler \(q\).

Expected Information Gain (EIG) and BOED

The EIG for a proposed experiment at \(\mathbf{x}^*\) is the expected KL divergence from prior to posterior:

$$\text{EIG}(\mathbf{x}^*) = \mathbb{E}_{p(y^*|\mathbf{x}^*)}\!\left[D_{KL}\!\left(p(\boldsymbol{\theta} \mid y^*, \mathbf{x}^*) \;\big\|\; p(\boldsymbol{\theta})\right)\right]$$

For a Gaussian surrogate, EIG reduces to expected posterior variance reduction. The Expected Improvement (EI) acquisition function in Bayesian optimisation (Ch. 15) is a special case where the parameter of interest is the location of the global optimum.

Cross-Entropy as a Loss Function

$$\mathcal{L}_{CE} = -\sum_{i=1}^n \sum_{k=1}^K y_{ik} \log \hat{p}_{ik}$$

Minimising cross-entropy is equivalent to MLE under a categorical distribution. Binary cross-entropy (\(K=2\)) is the default loss for binary classifiers. If your model minimises cross-entropy, it is doing MLE — full stop.

Lesson — Why Cholesky for GP
GP training requires \(\mathbf{K}^{-1}\mathbf{y}\) and \(\log|\mathbf{K}|\) where \(\mathbf{K}\in\mathbb{R}^{n\times n}\) is the kernel matrix. Computing \(\mathbf{K}^{-1}\) directly is numerically unstable. Cholesky: \(\mathbf{K} = \mathbf{L}\mathbf{L}^\top\). Then \(\mathbf{K}^{-1}\mathbf{y}\) is solved by forward/backward substitution (\(\mathcal{O}(n^2)\)) and \(\log|\mathbf{K}| = 2\sum_i \log L_{ii}\). This is the standard GP training algorithm.

SVD and Dimensionality

  • PCA: principal components are the columns of \(\mathbf{V}\) (right singular vectors). Variance explained by component \(j\): \(\sigma_j^2 / \sum_k \sigma_k^2\).
  • Rank: near-zero singular values indicate near-collinearity — a numerical warning in regression and kernel methods.
  • Low-rank approximation: best rank-\(k\) approximation by Eckart-Young theorem: \(\mathbf{U}_k\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_k\mathbf{V}_k^\top\). Basis of sparse GP inducing point methods.

Positive Definiteness and Kernels

Kernel matrices must be positive definite (PD) for GP regression to be well-posed. Valid kernels (Matérn-5/2, RBF, polynomial) guarantee PD matrices for any input set. Testing PD: Cholesky factorisation succeeds iff the matrix is PD. A failed Cholesky is a numerical red flag — add jitter \(\epsilon \mathbf{I}\).

6. Hypothesis Testing and Confidence Intervals

Framework

  1. Null hypothesis \(H_0\): conservative claim (e.g., "Model A and B have equal test RMSE")
  2. p-value: \(P(\text{data as extreme} \mid H_0)\). Not the probability that \(H_0\) is true.
  3. Significance level \(\alpha = 0.05\): pre-specified false positive rate.
Pitfall — p-values in model comparison
With \(n = 10{,}000\) test points, an RMSE difference of 0.001% may be statistically significant (\(p < 0.05\)) but practically meaningless. Always report both the p-value and the effect size (absolute improvement). A model that is 0.3% better on a regulatory metric matters; 0.001% better does not.

Tests Used in the Handbook

Paired t-test for comparing two models on the same test set:

$$t = \frac{\bar{d}}{\hat{s}_d / \sqrt{n}}, \qquad \bar{d} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n (e_i^A - e_i^B)$$

Under \(H_0\): \(t \sim t_{n-1}\).

Diebold-Mariano test (Ch. 9 — time series): accounts for temporal autocorrelation in forecast errors, which the paired t-test ignores. Use this for any time series model comparison.

Kolmogorov-Smirnov test: checks whether two distributions are the same. Used in concept drift detection (Ch. 8) and reliability analysis.

Confidence vs. Prediction Intervals

CI for OLS coefficient \(\hat{\beta}_j\): \(\hat{\beta}_j \pm t_{n-p,\,\alpha/2} \cdot \hat{\mathrm{se}}(\hat{\beta}_j)\).

PI for a new observation \(y^*\):

$$\hat{y}^* \pm t_{n-p,\,\alpha/2} \cdot \hat{\sigma}\sqrt{1 + \mathbf{x}^{*\top}(\mathbf{X}^\top\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{x}^*}$$
Rule — CI vs. PI in virtual sensors
A confidence interval covers the mean prediction — insufficient for regulatory compliance. A prediction interval covers a new observation — the PI is always wider and is what you report for virtual sensor deployment. Conflating the two leads to systematically overconfident uncertainty claims.

7. Model Evaluation Metrics

Regression Metrics

$$\text{RMSE} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{n}\sum_i(y_i-\hat{y}_i)^2}, \quad \text{MAE} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_i|y_i-\hat{y}_i|, \quad R^2 = 1 - \frac{\sum_i(y_i-\hat{y}_i)^2}{\sum_i(y_i-\bar{y})^2}$$
Rule — Which regression metric to use
MetricUse whenEngineering example
RMSEErrors in original units; penalises large errorsNOx (g/km): target RMSE < 0.05 g/km
MAERobust to outliers; median-likeCatalyst conversion: MAE < 2%
MAPERelative error; target > 0Fuel consumption: MAPE < 3%
\(R^2\)Quick check of explained variance\(R^2 > 0.99\): excellent for DOE surrogate
Max ErrorRegulatory limit at any pointOBD: max error < 30% of limit
\(R^2 = 1\): perfect. \(R^2 = 0\): model predicts mean everywhere. \(R^2 < 0\): model is worse than the mean — likely data leakage.

Classification Metrics

$$\text{Precision} = \frac{TP}{TP+FP}, \quad \text{Recall} = \frac{TP}{TP+FN}, \quad F_1 = 2\cdot\frac{\text{Prec}\cdot\text{Recall}}{\text{Prec}+\text{Recall}}$$

ROC-AUC: probability that the model ranks a random positive higher than a random negative. AUC = 0.5: random; AUC = 1.0: perfect. For fault detection (e.g., catalyst fault), set Recall ≥ 0.95 first, then tune threshold — AUC summarises discrimination ability independently of threshold.

Calibration: a classifier predicting 80% probability of compressor surge must be right 80% of the time. Test with reliability diagrams and Expected Calibration Error (ECE). Uncalibrated models are not safe for engineering deployment.

Pitfall — Random KFold on engineering data
Engineering datasets almost always have temporal or batch structure. Using random KFold splits temporally adjacent points into train and validation sets — the model sees the "future" during training. This gives optimistic RMSE estimates by 20–60%. Use GroupKFold with groups = measurement campaign ID, test rig, or batch number.

9. Weak Correlations and the Scientific Method

Example — Hidden signal in the wrong coordinate system
Rocket engine dataset, \(n=180\) hot-fire points. Pearson correlation between injector orifice diameter \(d_{or}\) and specific impulse \(I_{sp}\): \(r = 0.09\) (\(p = 0.24\), not significant). Naive conclusion: discard \(d_{or}\).

Physical hypothesis: the relevant variable is not \(d_{or}\) but injection velocity \(v_{inj} = \dot{m}/(\rho A_{or}) \propto d_{or}^{-2}\). Recompute: \(r(v_{inj}, I_{sp}) = 0.61\) (\(p < 10^{-18}\)).

Lesson: run EDA in dimensionless / physics-motivated coordinates (Ch. 3), not raw sensor units. A Pearson correlation of \(r = 0.09\) in the wrong space is a strong signal of \(r = 0.61\) in the right space.
Rule — Interpreting weak correlations
  1. Effect size, not p-value. \(r^2 = 0.02\) explains 2% of variance. Whether that matters is an application decision, not a statistics decision.
  2. Conditional correlation. A weak marginal \(r(X,Y)\) may hide a strong \(r(X,Y|Z)\). Check partial correlations for known physical confounders.
  3. Physical plausibility screen. If a weak correlation has a physical explanation, invest in testing it. If not, suspect confounding.
  4. Bootstrap CIs for small \(n\). For \(n < 100\): report 95% bootstrap CI on \(r\) rather than a p-value. \(r = 0.25\) with CI [0.05, 0.42] is informative.
  5. Mutual information \(I(X;Y)\) detects nonlinear dependence that Pearson \(r\) misses. Near-zero \(r\) with high MI is the signature of a nonlinear physical relationship. Use sklearn.feature_selection.mutual_info_regression (Kraskov k-NN estimator).
Pitfall — Theorising before data
SHAP values, permutation importance, and correlation coefficients all measure predictive relevance in a fitted model — not physical causation. Assigning physical meaning to them without a testable hypothesis is the "theorise before data" error in reverse. The scientific method demands: observe → hypothesise → predict → test → revise. The ML model is an instrument in steps 2–4, not a replacement for the method.

Correlation vs. Causation

Correlation is the input to the scientific method, not its output. A high correlation is a hypothesis prompt: "investigate whether \(X\) causes \(Y\) through mechanism \(M\)." Causal inference (Ch. 8 — Interpretability) then tests that hypothesis. A low correlation is also a hypothesis prompt: "is there a coordinate transformation that reveals the true relationship?"

Chapter Summary

Key takeaways
  • Bayes theorem is the unifying framework. OLS = MLE (Gaussian noise). Ridge = MAP (Gaussian prior). Lasso = MAP (Laplace prior). GP regression = Full Bayes over function space.
  • Bias-variance decomposition: diagnose failure before choosing a fix. The correct action depends on whether you have high bias, high variance, or irreducible noise.
  • Never invert a matrix directly — always use Cholesky for PD systems. This is not a style preference; it is a numerical stability requirement.
  • GroupKFold is the default for engineering data. Random KFold on batch/temporal data is a systematic error.
  • PI ≠ CI: virtual sensor deployment requires prediction intervals, not confidence intervals on the mean.
  • Mutual information screens for nonlinear relationships that Pearson misses. Physics-motivated coordinates surface hidden strong correlations.
