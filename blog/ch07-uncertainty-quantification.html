TL;DR

  • Epistemic uncertainty (from limited data) can be reduced by collecting more data. Aleatoric uncertainty (irreducible measurement noise) cannot. Confusing them gives wrong safety margins.
  • Conformal prediction is the only method with a distribution-free, finite-sample coverage guarantee — mandatory for regulatory submissions (EASA, ISO 26262, ISO 21448).
  • Deep ensembles ($M = 5$) consistently outperform MC Dropout, BNNs, and temperature scaling in calibration across all benchmarks. Default choice for production NN uncertainty.
  • MC Dropout does not grow uncertainty outside the training domain — always supplement with an OOD detector (Mahalanobis distance, energy score).
  • Calibration check: plot the reliability diagram. Empirical coverage at level $\alpha$ must equal $\alpha$. Systematic under-coverage in any region is a deployment blocker.
  • GP posterior is exact UQ under the GP model — grows automatically with distance from training data. The gold standard for small-to-medium data regimes (<10,000 points).
  • Always evaluate probabilistic forecasts with CRPS (Continuous Ranked Probability Score) — it simultaneously scores sharpness and calibration in a single proper scoring rule.

1. Why UQ Matters: Three Engineering Scenarios

A surrogate model without uncertainty quantification is, at best, a black box that gives point estimates. In engineering, point estimates without uncertainty are not decisions — they are guesses with legal exposure. Three scenarios that make this concrete:

Scenario A: Compressor Surge Margin

A surrogate predicts surge margin = 8%. Without a prediction interval, the engineer cannot determine whether 8% is a confident result or whether the true value could be 3%–14%. With a 95% PI of [6.1%, 9.9%], the lower bound informs how much operating margin to leave. With a 95% PI of [3.2%, 12.8%], the engineer derates the operating point. Same point estimate — vastly different engineering decisions.

Scenario B: NOx Virtual Sensor at Euro 6d Limit

Virtual NOx sensor predicts 0.38 g/kWh (Euro 6d limit: 0.40 g/kWh). Without UQ: compliance pass. With 95% PI [0.31, 0.47]: upper bound exceeds the limit — flag for physical PEMS verification. The regulatory framework (UN/ECE R49, EU 715/2007) requires demonstrated compliance at the upper bound of measurement uncertainty, not at the point estimate.

Scenario C: Bayesian Optimisation Exploration

A GP surrogate for FEM simulation is queried at a new candidate design. Posterior $\sigma^*(\mathbf{x}) > 3\sigma_f$ signals that prediction uncertainty is larger than the improvement potential — any "improvement" is within the uncertainty band and might not be real. The Expected Improvement acquisition function automatically downweights this candidate. Without UQ, gradient-based optimisation would naively evaluate an expensive simulation at an informationally worthless point.

2. Uncertainty Taxonomy: Epistemic, Aleatoric, Distributional

2.1 Epistemic Uncertainty (Reducible)

Arises from limited data and/or model choice. Measures what the model does not know. Mathematically, it is the variance of the posterior predictive distribution over model parameters:

$$U_{epistemic}(\mathbf{x}^*) = \mathbb{V}_{p(\boldsymbol{\theta}|\mathcal{D})}[\mathbb{E}[y|\mathbf{x}^*, \boldsymbol{\theta}]]$$

In GP notation: $U_{epistemic}(\mathbf{x}^*) = k(\mathbf{x}^*, \mathbf{x}^*) - \mathbf{k}_*^\top(\mathbf{K} + \sigma_n^2\mathbf{I})^{-1}\mathbf{k}_*$. Zero at training points, increases with distance from training data, and reduces as more data is added.

2.2 Aleatoric Uncertainty (Irreducible)

Arises from inherent stochasticity in the data-generating process: measurement noise, unresolved physical processes, sensor quantisation. Adding more training data does not reduce it. In GP notation: $U_{aleatoric} = \sigma_n^2$ (the noise kernel). Can only be reduced by improving instrumentation.

$$U_{total}(\mathbf{x}^*) = \underbrace{U_{epistemic}(\mathbf{x}^*)}_{\text{reducible}} + \underbrace{U_{aleatoric}}_{\text{irreducible}} = \sigma^{*2}_{model} + \sigma_n^2$$

2.3 Distributional (OOD) Uncertainty

A third category distinct from the above: the input $\mathbf{x}^*$ lies outside the training distribution entirely. Most UQ methods underestimate uncertainty in this regime. Requires a dedicated out-of-distribution detector (see Section 10). GP posteriors are the exception — epistemic uncertainty grows automatically, though the growth rate depends on the kernel length-scale, not on the actual density of training data.

Pitfall — Conflating Aleatoric and Epistemic
Many production ML systems report only total uncertainty $\sigma^{*2}_{total}$ and present it as if it were all reducible. This causes two errors: (1) engineers collect more data expecting uncertainty to decrease — it doesn't, because the floor is aleatoric; (2) active learning acquisition functions that should target high-epistemic regions instead target high-aleatoric regions (noise hot-spots), wasting labelling budget. Always decompose uncertainty before using it for decisions.

3. Gaussian Process Posterior: The Calibrated Baseline

The GP posterior predictive distribution is Gaussian and analytically exact under the GP model assumptions:

$$p(y^* | \mathbf{x}^*, \mathcal{D}) = \mathcal{N}\!\bigl(\mu^*(\mathbf{x}^*),\; \sigma^{*2}(\mathbf{x}^*)\bigr)$$ $$\mu^*(\mathbf{x}^*) = \mathbf{k}_*^\top (\mathbf{K} + \sigma_n^2\mathbf{I})^{-1}\mathbf{y}$$ $$\sigma^{*2}(\mathbf{x}^*) = k(\mathbf{x}^*, \mathbf{x}^*) - \mathbf{k}_*^\top (\mathbf{K} + \sigma_n^2\mathbf{I})^{-1}\mathbf{k}_*$$

The 95% prediction interval is $[\mu^* - 1.96\sigma^*,\; \mu^* + 1.96\sigma^*]$ (total uncertainty including noise: $\sigma^{*2}_{pred} = \sigma^{*2}(\mathbf{x}^*) + \sigma_n^2$).

3.1 Key Properties of GP Uncertainty

  • Calibrated by construction: if the kernel and noise level are correctly specified, 95% PI contains 95% of test points.
  • Zero epistemic uncertainty at training points: $\sigma^*(\mathbf{x}_i) = 0$ for noiseless GP. This is the correct behaviour — no epistemic uncertainty where we have exact observations.
  • Grows with distance from training data: the rate of growth depends on the kernel length-scale. Long-scale RBF kernels grow slowly; Matérn-1/2 grows faster.
  • Encodes active learning: points with high $\sigma^*$ are the most informative to query next (maximum entropy sampling, or GP-UCB acquisition).

3.2 Hyperparameter Uncertainty

Standard GP uses point estimates for kernel hyperparameters $\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}} = \arg\max\log p(\mathbf{y}|\mathbf{X},\boldsymbol{\theta})$ (type-II MLE / marginal likelihood optimisation). This underestimates total uncertainty because it ignores hyperparameter uncertainty. Fully Bayesian treatment requires MCMC (HMC with NUTS in GPyTorch/NumPyro) or an approximation (Student-t process, sparse spectral GP). For engineering surrogates with $n < 500$, the marginal likelihood posterior over $\boldsymbol{\theta}$ is often broad — run multi-start optimisation (10+ restarts) to avoid poor local optima.

import gpflow
import numpy as np

# Heteroscedastic GP with trainable noise (recommended over fixed sigma_n)
X_train = np.array(...)  # shape (n, d)
y_train = np.array(...)  # shape (n, 1)

kernel = gpflow.kernels.Matern52(lengthscales=np.ones(X_train.shape[1]))
model = gpflow.models.GPR(data=(X_train, y_train), kernel=kernel)

# Log-transform if y is positive-valued (e.g. NOx, force)
y_log = np.log1p(y_train)
model_log = gpflow.models.GPR(data=(X_train, y_log), kernel=kernel)

from gpflow.optimizers import Scipy
opt = Scipy()
opt.minimize(model.training_loss, model.trainable_variables, options={'maxiter': 500})

# Posterior prediction
mu, var = model.predict_y(X_test)   # includes noise variance
mu, sig2 = model.predict_f(X_test)  # epistemic only (latent function)
pi_95_lo = mu - 1.96 * np.sqrt(var)
pi_95_hi = mu + 1.96 * np.sqrt(var)

3.3 Scalable GP Approximations

Exact GP inference is $\mathcal{O}(n^3)$ in time and $\mathcal{O}(n^2)$ in memory. For $n > 5000$, use:

  • SVGP (Sparse Variational GP): $m \ll n$ inducing points, $\mathcal{O}(nm^2)$ complexity. Best variational approximation preserving posterior uncertainty structure. GPflow / GPyTorch.
  • KISS-GP (Kernel Interpolation for Scalable GPs): structured kernel interpolation with regular inducing grid, $\mathcal{O}(n)$ complexity on GPU. GPyTorch.
  • Random Fourier Features (RFF): approximate RBF/Matérn kernel with $D$ Fourier features, train linear model. $\mathcal{O}(nD)$ with $D \sim 1000$–5000. Loses exact GP posterior structure.

4. Monte Carlo Dropout

Train MLP with dropout rate $p$. At test time, run $T$ stochastic forward passes with dropout active (Gal & Ghahramani, 2016). The sample mean and variance approximate the posterior predictive mean and variance:

$$\hat{\mu}(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{1}{T}\sum_{t=1}^T \hat{y}_t(\mathbf{x}), \qquad \hat{\sigma}^2(\mathbf{x}) \approx \frac{1}{T}\sum_{t=1}^T \hat{y}_t^2 - \hat{\mu}^2 + \frac{p}{1-p}\|\mathbf{w}\|^2 \cdot \tau^{-1}$$

where $\tau$ is the model precision (linked to regularisation strength). The second term is the contribution from model weights to total uncertainty.

import torch
import torch.nn as nn

class MCDropoutNet(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, d_in, d_out, hidden=128, p_drop=0.1):
        super().__init__()
        self.net = nn.Sequential(
            nn.Linear(d_in, hidden), nn.GELU(), nn.Dropout(p_drop),
            nn.Linear(hidden, hidden), nn.GELU(), nn.Dropout(p_drop),
            nn.Linear(hidden, d_out)
        )

    def forward(self, x):
        return self.net(x)

    def predict_with_uncertainty(self, x, T=100):
        """MC Dropout inference: T stochastic forward passes."""
        self.train()  # activate dropout
        with torch.no_grad():
            preds = torch.stack([self(x) for _ in range(T)])  # (T, batch, out)
        self.eval()
        mu  = preds.mean(0)
        var = preds.var(0)
        return mu, var

Use $T = 50$–200 passes. At $T = 100$, inference cost is $100\times$ a single forward pass — often prohibitive for embedded/real-time deployment. Prefer deep ensembles ($M = 5$, $5\times$ cost) over MC Dropout ($T = 100$, $100\times$ cost) on latency-constrained systems.

Pitfall 1 — MC Dropout Fails Outside the Training Domain
MC Dropout uncertainty does not grow significantly outside the training domain. At an OOD point $\mathbf{x}^*$, all $T$ dropout networks produce similar (confidently wrong) outputs — the variance between them is small. Unlike GPs, MC Dropout cannot detect extrapolation. Always supplement with a Mahalanobis distance or energy-based OOD detector (see Section 10).
Pitfall 2 — Dropout Rate is Not a Free Parameter
Fixed $p = 0.5$ (common in NLP) produces over-wide uncertainty in regression tasks. Tune $p \in [0.02, 0.20]$ by maximising the Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) on a held-out calibration set — not by cross-validation on RMSE, which ignores calibration quality. A well-tuned $p = 0.05$ often outperforms $p = 0.2$ in calibration.

5. Deep Ensembles

Train $M$ networks with identical architecture but independent random weight initialisations (and optionally different data orderings). Each network outputs a Gaussian: $(\mu^{(m)}(\mathbf{x}), \sigma^{(m)2}(\mathbf{x}))$ using a Gaussian NLL loss head. The ensemble predictive distribution is a mixture of Gaussians:

$$\mu^*(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{1}{M}\sum_{m=1}^M \mu^{(m)}(\mathbf{x})$$ $$\sigma^{*2}(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{1}{M}\sum_{m=1}^M \left[\sigma^{(m)2}(\mathbf{x}) + \mu^{(m)2}(\mathbf{x})\right] - \mu^{*2}(\mathbf{x})$$

The second variance component captures disagreement between ensemble members — this is the ensemble's proxy for epistemic uncertainty. The first component averages the predicted aleatoric variances.

import torch
import torch.nn as nn

class GaussianHead(nn.Module):
    """Network that outputs (mu, log_sigma) for Gaussian NLL loss."""
    def __init__(self, d_in, hidden=128):
        super().__init__()
        self.backbone = nn.Sequential(
            nn.Linear(d_in, hidden), nn.GELU(),
            nn.Linear(hidden, hidden), nn.GELU()
        )
        self.mu_head    = nn.Linear(hidden, 1)
        self.log_s_head = nn.Linear(hidden, 1)  # log(sigma), avoid negative sigma

    def forward(self, x):
        h = self.backbone(x)
        mu = self.mu_head(h).squeeze(-1)
        log_s = self.log_s_head(h).squeeze(-1).clamp(-6, 2)  # sigma in [e^-6, e^2]
        return mu, log_s.exp()

def gaussian_nll_loss(mu, sigma, target):
    return (0.5 * ((target - mu) / sigma)**2 + sigma.log()).mean()

# Train M independent models
M = 5
ensemble = [GaussianHead(d_in=X_train.shape[1]) for _ in range(M)]
# Train each independently with gaussian_nll_loss ...

# Ensemble prediction
def ensemble_predict(ensemble, x):
    mus, sigs = [], []
    for net in ensemble:
        net.eval()
        with torch.no_grad():
            mu, sig = net(x)
        mus.append(mu); sigs.append(sig)
    mus = torch.stack(mus); sigs = torch.stack(sigs)
    mu_star  = mus.mean(0)
    var_star = (sigs**2 + mus**2).mean(0) - mu_star**2
    return mu_star, var_star.sqrt()

Deep ensembles ($M=5$) consistently outperform MC Dropout, variational BNNs, and temperature scaling in calibration quality across tabular and structured data benchmarks (Lakshminarayanan et al., 2017; Ovadia et al., 2019). The main practical drawback is $M\times$ training and storage cost.

6. Laplace Approximation and Bayesian Last Layer

6.1 Full Laplace Approximation

The Laplace approximation fits a Gaussian to the posterior $p(\boldsymbol{\theta}|\mathcal{D})$ centred at the MAP estimate $\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}$, using the inverse Hessian of the negative log-posterior as covariance:

$$p(\boldsymbol{\theta}|\mathcal{D}) \approx \mathcal{N}(\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}},\; \mathbf{H}^{-1}), \qquad \mathbf{H} = -\nabla^2_{\boldsymbol{\theta}} \log p(\boldsymbol{\theta}|\mathcal{D})\bigl|_{\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}}$$

The predictive distribution is then a Gaussian with variance propagated through the network Jacobian. Computing and storing $\mathbf{H}^{-1}$ for a large NN is impractical — use Kronecker-factored Laplace (KFAC) or diagonal Laplace as approximations.

6.2 Bayesian Last Layer (BLL)

A practical compromise: treat all layers except the last as a deterministic feature extractor, and apply exact Bayesian linear regression on the final layer features. Gives analytically tractable posterior with a single forward pass:

$$p(\mathbf{w}|\mathcal{D}) = \mathcal{N}(\hat{\mathbf{w}},\, \mathbf{A}^{-1}), \quad \mathbf{A} = \frac{1}{\sigma_n^2}\boldsymbol{\Phi}^\top\boldsymbol{\Phi} + \frac{1}{\sigma_w^2}\mathbf{I}$$ $$p(y^*|\mathbf{x}^*, \mathcal{D}) = \mathcal{N}(\hat{\mathbf{w}}^\top\boldsymbol{\phi}^*,\, \boldsymbol{\phi}^{*\top}\mathbf{A}^{-1}\boldsymbol{\phi}^* + \sigma_n^2)$$

where $\boldsymbol{\Phi} \in \mathbb{R}^{n\times d_{last}}$ is the matrix of last-layer activations and $\boldsymbol{\phi}^*$ is the activation at the test point. BLL has inference cost $\mathcal{O}(nd_{last}^2)$ — tractable for $d_{last} \leq 512$.

import torch
import torch.nn as nn
import numpy as np

class BayesianLastLayer:
    """Bayesian linear regression on top of a frozen neural network."""
    def __init__(self, backbone, sigma_n=0.1, sigma_w=1.0):
        self.backbone = backbone.eval()
        self.sigma_n = sigma_n
        self.sigma_w = sigma_w
        self.A_inv = None
        self.w_hat = None

    def fit(self, X, y):
        with torch.no_grad():
            Phi = self.backbone(X).numpy()  # (n, d_last)
        n, d = Phi.shape
        A = (1/self.sigma_n**2) * Phi.T @ Phi + (1/self.sigma_w**2) * np.eye(d)
        self.A_inv = np.linalg.inv(A)
        self.w_hat = (1/self.sigma_n**2) * self.A_inv @ Phi.T @ y.numpy()

    def predict(self, X_test):
        with torch.no_grad():
            phi = self.backbone(X_test).numpy()  # (m, d_last)
        mu  = phi @ self.w_hat
        var = np.einsum('ij,jk,ik->i', phi, self.A_inv, phi) + self.sigma_n**2
        return mu, np.sqrt(var)

7. Quantile Regression and Interval Scoring

7.1 Quantile Regression

Instead of predicting $\mathbb{E}[y|\mathbf{x}]$, quantile regression predicts the $\tau$-th quantile $Q_\tau(y|\mathbf{x})$ by minimising the pinball (tilted absolute) loss:

$$\mathcal{L}_{pinball}(\hat{q}_\tau, y) = \begin{cases} \tau(y - \hat{q}_\tau) & \text{if } y \geq \hat{q}_\tau \\ (1-\tau)(\hat{q}_\tau - y) & \text{if } y < \hat{q}_\tau \end{cases}$$

Train two separate models for $\tau = 0.05$ and $\tau = 0.95$ to get 90% prediction intervals. Advantages: makes no distributional assumption (not Gaussian, not symmetric); directly targets the interval endpoints. Disadvantage: no decomposition into epistemic vs aleatoric uncertainty; no OOD detection capability.

7.2 Interval Score (Proper Scoring for Intervals)

To evaluate prediction intervals (PIs) rather than full distributions, the Interval Score (IS) simultaneously penalises width and mis-coverage:

$$IS_\alpha([\hat{l}, \hat{u}], y) = (\hat{u} - \hat{l}) + \frac{2}{\alpha}\bigl[({\hat{l} - y})_+ + ({y - \hat{u}})_+\bigr]$$

where $\alpha = 1 - (1-\alpha_{coverage}) = $ nominal miscoverage rate. A PI that is wide but perfectly calibrated may score worse than a narrow but well-calibrated one. Lower IS is better.

8. Conformal Prediction: Distribution-Free Coverage Guarantee

Conformal prediction (Vovk et al., 2005; Angelopoulos & Bates, 2023) provides a finite-sample, distribution-free marginal coverage guarantee for any black-box model — the only UQ method with this property.

$$P(y_{n+1} \in C(\mathbf{x}_{n+1})) \geq 1 - \alpha \quad \text{for any model } \hat{f} \text{ and any distribution}$$

8.1 Split Conformal Prediction (Inductive)

Split Conformal Algorithm
  1. Split data: training set $\mathcal{D}_{train}$, calibration set $\mathcal{D}_{cal}$ (hold out 10–20% of data).
  2. Train model $\hat{f}$ on $\mathcal{D}_{train}$.
  3. Compute conformity scores on calibration set: $s_i = |y_i - \hat{f}(\mathbf{x}_i)|$ (residual magnitude). Other scores are possible: normalised residual $|y_i - \hat{f}(\mathbf{x}_i)| / \hat{\sigma}(\mathbf{x}_i)$ (CQR), signed residual for asymmetric intervals.
  4. Compute the $(1-\alpha)(1 + 1/n_{cal})$ quantile of the calibration scores: $\hat{q}_\alpha$.
  5. Prediction interval for new point $\mathbf{x}_{n+1}$: $C(\mathbf{x}_{n+1}) = [\hat{f}(\mathbf{x}_{n+1}) - \hat{q}_\alpha,\; \hat{f}(\mathbf{x}_{n+1}) + \hat{q}_\alpha]$.
Guarantee: $P(y_{n+1} \in C(\mathbf{x}_{n+1})) \geq 1-\alpha$ for any $\hat{f}$, any distribution, any sample size. 
import numpy as np

def split_conformal_predict(y_cal, y_hat_cal, alpha, y_hat_test):
    """
    Split conformal prediction intervals.
    alpha: miscoverage level (e.g., 0.1 for 90% coverage)
    Returns: (lower, upper) arrays for test predictions.
    """
    n = len(y_cal)
    scores = np.abs(y_cal - y_hat_cal)                        # conformity scores
    q_level = np.ceil((1 - alpha) * (n + 1)) / n             # inflated quantile
    q_hat = np.quantile(scores, q_level, method='higher')     # conformal quantile

    return y_hat_test - q_hat, y_hat_test + q_hat

# Usage
lo, hi = split_conformal_predict(y_cal, model.predict(X_cal), alpha=0.10,
                                  y_hat_test=model.predict(X_test))
empirical_coverage = np.mean((y_test >= lo) & (y_test <= hi))
print(f"Empirical coverage: {empirical_coverage:.3f}")  # should be ≥ 0.90

8.2 Conformalized Quantile Regression (CQR)

Standard split conformal produces symmetric, constant-width intervals. CQR uses the conformity score $s_i = \max(\hat{q}_{lo}(\mathbf{x}_i) - y_i,\; y_i - \hat{q}_{hi}(\mathbf{x}_i))$ where $\hat{q}_{lo}$, $\hat{q}_{hi}$ are fitted quantile regressors. This produces adaptive-width intervals — narrower in well-predicted regions, wider in uncertain ones — while preserving the coverage guarantee.

8.3 Weighted Conformal Prediction (Covariate Shift)

When the test distribution differs from calibration (covariate shift), standard conformal loses its coverage guarantee. Weighted conformal uses importance weights $w_i = p_{test}(\mathbf{x}_i) / p_{cal}(\mathbf{x}_i)$ (estimated by a classifier) to reweight the calibration scores. This restores the coverage guarantee under mild covariate shift — relevant for turbomachinery models validated on bench data but deployed on different fleet configurations.

Regulatory Applications of Conformal Prediction
  • EASA AI Roadmap / CM-0064: requires demonstrable coverage guarantees for AI systems in aviation. Split conformal is the natural candidate — it makes no modelling assumptions and works with any existing model.
  • ISO 26262 ASIL-B: functional safety for automotive. Conformal PIs can contribute to the evidence of safe-state detection in virtual sensor chains.
  • ISO 21448 (SOTIF): safety of the intended functionality. CQR with OOD-triggered widening addresses SOTIF's concern about ML performance in unknown scenarios.

9. Calibration Metrics: CRPS, NLL, Reliability Diagrams

9.1 Reliability Diagrams (Calibration Plots)

For a probabilistic regression model, calibration means: of all predictions where the model assigns 90% coverage, 90% of true values should fall inside the predicted interval. Construct the reliability diagram by:

  1. For $K$ coverage levels $\alpha_k \in \{0.05, 0.10, \ldots, 0.95\}$:
  2. Compute the empirical fraction of test points inside the $\alpha_k$ PI: $\hat{c}_k = |\{i : y_i \in C_{\alpha_k}(\mathbf{x}_i)\}| / n_{test}$
  3. Plot $\hat{c}_k$ (y-axis) vs $\alpha_k$ (x-axis). A perfectly calibrated model lies on the diagonal.
  • Above diagonal: over-confident — intervals are too narrow
  • Below diagonal: under-confident — intervals are unnecessarily wide
  • Crossing diagonal: calibration failure depends on level; investigate regionally
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

def reliability_diagram(mu, sigma, y_true, alphas=None):
    """Plot reliability diagram for Gaussian predictive distribution."""
    if alphas is None:
        alphas = np.linspace(0.05, 0.95, 19)
    from scipy.stats import norm
    empirical = []
    for a in alphas:
        z = norm.ppf(1 - a/2)
        lo, hi = mu - z*sigma, mu + z*sigma
        empirical.append(np.mean((y_true >= lo) & (y_true <= hi)))

    plt.figure(figsize=(5, 5))
    plt.plot([0, 1], [0, 1], 'k--', label='Perfect calibration')
    plt.plot(alphas, empirical, 'o-', color='steelblue', label='Model')
    plt.fill_between(alphas, alphas, empirical,
                     alpha=0.15, color='red' if np.mean(empirical) < np.mean(alphas) else 'green')
    plt.xlabel('Nominal coverage $1-\\alpha$')
    plt.ylabel('Empirical coverage')
    plt.title('Reliability Diagram')
    plt.legend(); plt.tight_layout(); plt.show()

9.2 Continuous Ranked Probability Score (CRPS)

CRPS is a proper scoring rule for full probabilistic forecasts — it simultaneously rewards sharpness (narrow distributions) and penalises miscalibration. Lower is better. For a Gaussian predictive distribution $\mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$ observed at $y$:

$$CRPS(\mathcal{N}(\mu,\sigma^2), y) = \sigma\left[\frac{y-\mu}{\sigma}\left(2\Phi\!\left(\frac{y-\mu}{\sigma}\right)-1\right) + 2\phi\!\left(\frac{y-\mu}{\sigma}\right) - \frac{1}{\sqrt{\pi}}\right]$$

where $\Phi$ and $\phi$ are the standard normal CDF and PDF. CRPS generalises MAE (which is the CRPS for a Dirac delta forecast) and is comparable across different models and scales.

import numpy as np
from scipy.stats import norm

def crps_gaussian(mu, sigma, y):
    """CRPS for Gaussian predictive distribution."""
    z = (y - mu) / sigma
    return sigma * (z * (2*norm.cdf(z) - 1) + 2*norm.pdf(z) - 1/np.sqrt(np.pi))

# Usage
crps_per_sample = crps_gaussian(mu_pred, sigma_pred, y_test)
print(f"Mean CRPS: {crps_per_sample.mean():.4f}")
# Compare models by mean CRPS — lower is better

9.3 Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD)

For a Gaussian predictive distribution, NLPD is:

$$NLPD = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n \frac{1}{2}\ln(2\pi\sigma_i^2) + \frac{(y_i - \mu_i)^2}{2\sigma_i^2}$$

NLPD is also a proper scoring rule. It is more sensitive to the tails of the predicted distribution than CRPS. Use NLPD for tuning hyperparameters (dropout rate, ensemble size) and CRPS for reporting final model performance — CRPS is more robust to extreme outliers in $\sigma$.

9.4 Expected Calibration Error (ECE) and Calibration Width Score

  • ECE: $\sum_k w_k |\hat{c}_k - \alpha_k|$ — mean absolute deviation between empirical and nominal coverage, weighted by bin size.
  • Sharpness: mean interval width $\frac{1}{n}\sum_i (\hat{u}_i - \hat{l}_i)$. A model is "sharper" if its intervals are narrower at the same calibration level.
  • Report both: a model can be perfectly calibrated but infinitely wide (useless) or perfectly sharp but uncalibrated (dangerous).

10. Out-of-Distribution Detection

OOD detection identifies test inputs $\mathbf{x}^*$ that lie outside the training distribution — where any model's predictions are unreliable regardless of the UQ method used. It is a prerequisite for safe deployment of ML in engineering contexts.

10.1 Mahalanobis Distance

Compute the Mahalanobis distance from $\mathbf{x}^*$ to the training distribution $\mathcal{N}(\hat{\boldsymbol{\mu}}, \hat{\boldsymbol{\Sigma}})$ fitted to the training inputs (or to the penultimate-layer activations for NNs):

$$d_M(\mathbf{x}^*) = \sqrt{(\mathbf{x}^* - \hat{\boldsymbol{\mu}})^\top \hat{\boldsymbol{\Sigma}}^{-1} (\mathbf{x}^* - \hat{\boldsymbol{\mu}})}$$

Flag as OOD if $d_M(\mathbf{x}^*) > \chi^2_{d,0.95}$ (95th percentile of chi-squared with $d$ degrees of freedom). This is computationally cheap ($\mathcal{O}(d^2)$) and does not require modifying the model. Works best in the feature space of the penultimate layer (Lee et al., 2018).

import numpy as np

class MahalanobisOOD:
    def __init__(self, threshold_quantile=0.95):
        self.mu = None
        self.Sigma_inv = None
        self.threshold = None

    def fit(self, X_train_features):
        self.mu = X_train_features.mean(0)
        Sigma = np.cov(X_train_features.T)
        self.Sigma_inv = np.linalg.pinv(Sigma)
        # Set threshold from training distribution (for reference)
        d = self.mahalanobis(X_train_features)
        self.threshold = np.quantile(d, 0.99)  # flag top 1% as OOD

    def mahalanobis(self, X):
        delta = X - self.mu
        return np.sqrt(np.einsum('ij,jk,ik->i', delta, self.Sigma_inv, delta))

    def is_ood(self, X):
        return self.mahalanobis(X) > self.threshold

10.2 Energy Score

The energy score (Liu et al., 2020) uses the logits of a NN classifier to define an OOD score without requiring the training distribution to be Gaussian:

$$E(\mathbf{x}; T) = -T \ln \sum_{c=1}^C \exp(f_c(\mathbf{x})/T)$$

where $f_c(\mathbf{x})$ are the logits. In-distribution samples have lower energy (larger logit magnitudes); OOD samples tend to have higher energy. Temperature $T$ acts as a smoothing factor (typically $T = 1$). Works with existing classifiers without retraining.

10.3 Ensemble Disagreement

For deep ensembles, the variance between member predictions is a natural OOD indicator:

$$s_{OOD}(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{1}{M}\sum_{m=1}^M (\mu^{(m)}(\mathbf{x}) - \mu^*(\mathbf{x}))^2$$

High disagreement among ensemble members suggests the input is in a region where training data is sparse or absent. This is the most practically reliable OOD indicator for regression surrogates — GP $\sigma^*$ being a special case of this for the GP ensemble of kernel machines.

10.4 GP Uncertainty as OOD Score

For GP surrogates: $\sigma^*(\mathbf{x}^*) > 3\sigma_0$ (where $\sigma_0 = \sqrt{k(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{x})}$ is the prior standard deviation) indicates extrapolation beyond the training domain. This is the cleanest OOD signal available — no separate OOD module required. One more reason to prefer GPs for engineering surrogates where safety margins matter.

11. UQ for Active Learning

UQ is the engine of active learning: the model's uncertainty drives where to sample next, maximising information gain per experiment. This is crucial in engineering contexts where each measurement is expensive (physical test, CFD simulation, material synthesis).

11.1 Acquisition Functions

Acquisition FunctionFormulaBest Use

BALD (Bayesian Active Learning by Disagreement)$a(\mathbf{x}) = H[y|\mathbf{x},\mathcal{D}] - \mathbb{E}_{p(\boldsymbol{\theta}|\mathcal{D})}[H[y|\mathbf{x},\boldsymbol{\theta}]]$Pure epistemic uncertainty targeting
Integrated Mean Squared Error (IMSE)$a(\mathbf{x}) = \int \sigma^{*2}(\mathbf{x}')\,d\mathbf{x}'$ after adding $\mathbf{x}$Space-filling surrogate construction

11.2 Practical Active Learning Protocol

  1. Initial design: Latin Hypercube Sampling (LHS) with $5d$–$10d$ initial points ($d$ = input dimension).
  2. Fit GP on initial data.
  3. Select next point: maximise acquisition function over candidate pool (random or grid).
  4. Evaluate oracle: run the experiment/simulation at the selected point.
  5. Update GP: condition on new observation (online update — no full retraining needed).
  6. Stopping criterion: stop when max $\sigma^*(\mathbf{x}) < \epsilon_{tolerance}$ across the input domain, or when IMSE falls below target.

The Non-Negotiable UQ Checklist Before Deployment
  1. Plot the reliability diagram. Empirical coverage at 90% must be ≥ 90%. If under-coverage: apply conformal post-hoc calibration.
  2. Compute CRPS on a held-out test set and compare to a baseline (e.g., Gaussian with global mean and std). If CRPS is not better than baseline, your model's uncertainty estimates add no value.
  3. Deploy an OOD detector alongside the model. Log OOD flags in production. Define what happens when a flag is raised (alert, fallback to physics model, human review).
  4. Document the calibration set: its size, composition, and relationship to expected deployment distribution. A model calibrated on bench data deployed on field data with covariate shift loses its calibration guarantee.
  5. Separate epistemic from aleatoric uncertainty in any report that informs a safety-critical decision.

13. Key Takeaways

  • Epistemic vs. aleatoric: confusing them gives wrong safety margins and wastes labelling budget. Always decompose. GP is the only method that separates them analytically at prediction time.
  • Conformal prediction: mandatory for regulatory submissions. Distribution-free, finite-sample. Works with any black-box model. Use CQR for adaptive-width intervals.
  • Deep ensembles ($M=5$) beat MC Dropout in calibration quality consistently — and cost $5\times$ instead of $100\times$. Use them by default for production NNs.
  • MC Dropout does not detect OOD: supplement with Mahalanobis distance on penultimate-layer features. Never rely on MC Dropout alone for extrapolation detection.
  • CRPS is the right metric for probabilistic models: it jointly evaluates sharpness and calibration in a proper scoring rule. Report CRPS alongside RMSE.
  • Bayesian Last Layer: a practical compromise — deterministic backbone + exact Bayesian inference on the last layer. Single forward pass, analytically tractable posterior.
  • GP uncertainty is the gold standard for small data: calibrated by construction, encodes active learning acquisition, zero epistemic uncertainty at training points. Use sparse GP (SVGP) for $n > 2000$.
  • OOD detection is not optional: deploy a Mahalanobis, energy, or ensemble-disagreement detector alongside every production model. Define the response action for OOD flags before deployment, not after the first production incident.
