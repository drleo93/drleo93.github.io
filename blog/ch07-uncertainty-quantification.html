Ch 7: Uncertainty Quantification for Engineering Surrogates

Chapter 7 of 23  ·  ~25 min read  ·  A surrogate without UQ is dangerous
epistemic uncertainty aleatoric uncertainty conformal prediction deep ensembles MC Dropout GP posterior calibration prediction interval

TL;DR

  • Epistemic uncertainty (from limited data) can be reduced by collecting more data. Aleatoric uncertainty (measurement noise) cannot. A surrogate that confuses them gives wrong safety margins.
  • Conformal prediction provides distribution-free, finite-sample coverage guarantees — the only method valid for regulatory submissions (EASA, ISO 26262).
  • Deep ensembles (5–10 independently trained networks) consistently outperform MC Dropout in calibration quality across all benchmarks.
  • MC Dropout does not grow uncertainty outside the training domain — supplement with Mahalanobis distance OOD detection for extrapolation detection.
  • GP posterior uncertainty is exact under the GP prior: calibrated by construction, grows with distance from training data, zero at training points (up to noise).
  • Check calibration: empirical coverage at level \(1-\alpha\) should equal \(1-\alpha\). Plot reliability diagrams before any deployment.

1. Why UQ Matters

Three engineering scenarios where missing UQ causes failures:

  • Compressor map surrogate predicts surge margin = 8%. Without a PI, the engineer cannot determine whether this is confidently 8% or could be 3%–14%. With 95% PI [6.1%, 9.9%], the decision carries known risk.
  • LSTM NOx virtual sensor predicts 0.38 g/kWh (Euro 6d limit: 0.40). Without UQ: compliance pass. With 95% PI [0.31, 0.47]: upper bound exceeds limit — flag for additional measurement.
  • GP surrogate queried far from training data: posterior \(\sigma^*(\mathbf{x}) > 3\sigma_f\) signals unreliable prediction — request a new experiment before acting.

2. Epistemic vs. Aleatoric Uncertainty

Definitions

Epistemic uncertainty (from limited data): in GP notation:

$$U_{epistemic}(\mathbf{x}^*) = k_{**} - \mathbf{k}_*^\top(\mathbf{K} + \sigma_n^2\mathbf{I})^{-1}\mathbf{k}_*$$

Zero at training points. Increases with distance from training data. Can be reduced by collecting more data.

Aleatoric uncertainty (irreducible noise): \(U_{aleatoric} = \sigma_n^2\). Adding more training data does not reduce it. Can only be reduced by improving instrumentation.

Total: \(U_{total} = U_{epistemic} + U_{aleatoric}\).

3. GP Uncertainty: The Gold Standard

GP posterior predictive distribution is Gaussian:

$$p(y^* | \mathbf{x}^*, \mathcal{D}) = \mathcal{N}\!\bigl(\mu^*(\mathbf{x}^*),\; \sigma^{*2}(\mathbf{x}^*) + \sigma_n^2\bigr)$$

95% prediction interval: \([\mu^* - 1.96\sigma^*, \mu^* + 1.96\sigma^*]\).

Key properties: (1) calibrated by construction if the kernel is correctly specified; (2) high \(\sigma^*(\mathbf{x})\) identifies where to place the next experiment (active learning); (3) \(\sigma^* \to \sigma_n\) at training points.

Calibration check — empirical coverage should match nominal:

$$\hat{c}(\alpha) = \frac{1}{n_{test}}\sum_{i=1}^{n_{test}} \mathbf{1}\!\left[y_i \in \left[\mu^* \pm z_{1-\alpha/2}\sigma^*\right]\right] \approx 1-\alpha$$

4. Monte Carlo Dropout

Train MLP with dropout rate \(p\). At test time, run \(T\) stochastic forward passes with dropout active:

$$\hat{\mu}(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{1}{T}\sum_{t=1}^T \hat{y}_t(\mathbf{x}), \qquad \hat{\sigma}^2(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{1}{T}\sum_{t=1}^T \hat{y}_t^2 - \hat{\mu}^2 + \frac{p}{1-p}\|\mathbf{w}\|^2 \cdot \tau^{-1}$$

Use \(T = 50\text{–}200\) passes. At \(T=100\), inference cost is \(100\times\) a single forward pass — often unacceptable for embedded deployment.

Pitfall — Two MC Dropout calibration failures
  1. Overconfidence in extrapolation: MC Dropout uncertainty does not grow significantly outside the training domain — unlike GPs. Supplement with Mahalanobis distance to training data for OOD detection.
  2. Under-confidence in interpolation: with poorly tuned \(p\), MC Dropout produces uncertainty estimates too wide inside the training domain, creating overly conservative designs. Tune \(p \in [0.05, 0.20]\) via negative log-predictive density (NLPD) on a calibration set, not fixed at \(p=0.5\).

5. Deep Ensembles

Train \(M\) networks with identical architecture but independent random initialisations. Each outputs a Gaussian \((\mu^{(m)}, \sigma^{(m)2})\). The ensemble predictive distribution is a mixture of Gaussians:

$$\mu^*(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{1}{M}\sum_{m=1}^M \mu^{(m)}(\mathbf{x}), \qquad \sigma^{*2}(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{1}{M}\sum_{m=1}^M \left[\sigma^{(m)2} + \mu^{(m)2}\right] - \mu^{*2}$$

Deep ensembles (\(M=5\)) consistently outperform MC Dropout, Bayesian NNs, and temperature scaling in calibration quality across tabular and image benchmarks (Lakshminarayanan et al., 2017). Main drawback: \(M\times\) training and inference cost.

6. Conformal Prediction

Lesson — The only distribution-free coverage guarantee
Conformal prediction (Vovk et al.) provides a finite-sample, distribution-free coverage guarantee: for any black-box model \(\hat{f}\), it constructs prediction intervals \(C(\mathbf{x}^*)\) satisfying: $$P(y^* \in C(\mathbf{x}^*)) \geq 1 - \alpha$$ without assuming any distributional form.

Split conformal algorithm:
  1. Split data: calibration set \(\mathcal{D}_{cal}\), test set \(\mathcal{D}_{test}\)
  2. Compute conformity scores on calibration: \(s_i = |y_i - \hat{f}(\mathbf{x}_i)|\)
  3. Compute \(\hat{q} = \lceil(1-\alpha)(1 + 1/n_{cal})\rceil\)-th quantile of \(\{s_i\}\)
  4. Prediction interval: \([\hat{f}(\mathbf{x}^*) - \hat{q},\; \hat{f}(\mathbf{x}^*) + \hat{q}]\)
This is the method to use for EASA CM-0064, ISO 26262 ASIL-B, and any other regulatory submission requiring coverage guarantees.

Rule — UQ method by application
  • Regulatory submission (EASA/FAA/ISO): conformal prediction — the only method with distribution-free coverage guarantee
  • Active learning and Bayesian optimisation: GP posterior — exact epistemic uncertainty, grows in extrapolation
  • Production NN deployment: deep ensembles (\(M=5\)) — best calibration, most robust
  • Quick uncertainty estimate for NN: MC Dropout with \(p \in [0.05, 0.20]\) tuned by NLPD
  • Always: check empirical coverage with reliability diagrams before deployment

Chapter Summary

Key takeaways
  • Epistemic vs. aleatoric: confusing them gives wrong safety margins. GP separates them analytically. Deep ensembles conflate them (disagreement is partly epistemic, partly aleatoric).
  • Conformal prediction: mandatory for regulatory submissions. Distribution-free, finite-sample. Works with any black-box model.
  • Deep ensembles beat MC Dropout in calibration quality consistently. Use \(M=5\) as the default.
  • MC Dropout extrapolation failure: supplement with Mahalanobis distance OOD detector. Never rely on MC Dropout alone for safety-critical extrapolation detection.
  • Calibration check: plot reliability diagram. Empirical coverage at 90% should be ~90%. Systematic under-coverage in a specific input region indicates model misspecification there.
